A Lecture for the NYU Leadership Program

Mondragón as the Anchor in a Fragmented World

Geopolitics, Art, and the European Future — delivered before your visit to the Mondragón Cooperative Corporation

Prof. Luis Garvía Vega, PhD · NH Collection Madrid Suecia · NYU Leadership

Today you will board a bus. By dusk you will be walking through Arrasate-Mondragón, a narrow Basque valley that eighty years ago was too poor to feed its own children. What you will encounter there is not a museum, not a memorial, not an ideological monument. It is a working contradiction: a federation of more than ninety cooperatives, some seventy thousand people, owned not by shareholders but by the workers themselves, born inside the rubble of Europe's most violent century. These pages prepare your eyes.

Part IThe Fragmented World — Why Mondragón Matters Today

The contemporary news cycle teaches us to mistake noise for value. When Donald Trump touches down in Beijing, the planet's cameras follow every tarmac handshake. Meanwhile, Vladimir Putin moves a regiment, signs an obscure energy clause with a Central Asian republic, or quietly redraws a buffer state, and the press barely glances. The attention economy rewards spectacle and penalizes structure. Hold that asymmetry as you cross into the Basque Country: the cooperative you are about to encounter has thrived for nearly seven decades precisely because it has refused to be loud.

The Durand Line: a colonial scar across a living people

In 1893, a British civil servant named Sir Mortimer Durand drew a line across the Hindu Kush to separate the British Raj from Afghanistan. It separated, in fact, a people from itself. The Pashtuns — perhaps forty to fifty million strong today — were bisected into what is now Pakistan and Afghanistan. The line cut through villages, pastures, and the genealogical memory of a culture older than the empires that drew it.

And yet the Pashtuns survive, by something older than international law: a code called Pashtunwali — hospitality to the guest, sanctuary to the fugitive, council for the community. Unwritten, enforced by honor, it is what allowed a stateless people to remain a people. A detail worth holding: in Pashto, Taliban means quite simply "students" — the word for boys in the madrasas of refugee camps, children whose schools had been bombed into the dust.

You will recognize the pattern: a mountainous people, a border imposed from above, a culture that survives because the community refuses to let it die. The parallel with the Basques — split between France and Spain by a border they never drew — is not metaphorical. It is structural. Both peoples answer the same question in different idioms: how does a community survive when the maps are drawn by strangers?

Anchor to Mondragón

The Basque answer, in the twentieth century, was not Pashtunwali. It was the cooperative. But the impulse is the same: a community written out of the official map writes itself back in, with its own institutions, its own language, its own forms of mutual obligation. Mondragón is what Pashtunwali looks like when translated into industrial production.

The bottlenecks of empire

Geography is not destiny, but geography is patient. The Durand Line sits exactly where the next century's chokepoint is forming. The China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) — Beijing's roughly sixty-billion-dollar artery — runs from Kashgar down through Pakistani territory, hugging the eastern flank of the Durand Line, and terminates at the deep-water port of Gwadar on the Arabian Sea. From Gwadar, Chinese goods reach the Persian Gulf without crossing the Strait of Malacca, which the U.S. Navy can close in an afternoon. A parallel trade route, designed to escape American maritime supremacy.

Russia has its own bottleneck. The narrow land bridge between the Don and the Volga has been the strategic obsession of every Tsar, Commissar, and President in Moscow since Peter the Great. Whoever controls it controls Russia's access to the warm seas. The current war on Europe's eastern flank is, in one of its readings, a quarrel over precisely that bottleneck. Leaders who read maps slowly outlast leaders who read polls quickly.

From radial Madrid to closed valleys

Now turn your gaze west. Italy is a long bone of a country, with capitals of culture and capital scattered from Turin to Palermo; no single city dominates. Spain is the opposite. Spain is a wheel, and Madrid its hub. The roads, the high-speed rail, the airline networks — the very arteries of the state — radiate outward from the capital like the spokes of a star, by deliberate design since the Bourbon reforms of the eighteenth century.

This radial structure isolates the periphery. The Basque Country, tucked into the rain-soaked valleys of the Cantabrian range, was for centuries connected more easily to neighboring villages — and to the Atlantic — than to the capital that nominally administered it. Geography turned those closed valleys into pressure cookers of identity. When the next village is your only economic horizon, you either learn to cooperate with your neighbor or you starve with him. Basque solidarity was the mathematical consequence of the topography. Mondragón did not invent it.

Part IIArt as the Mirror of Disruption — The Crucible that Forged Mondragón

One cannot understand Mondragón without first understanding Guernica. And one cannot understand Guernica without understanding what happened to painting between 1820 and 1937 — how a continent learned to see its own catastrophe. Mondragón is the institutional answer to what that seeing revealed.

The death of realism

For five centuries the painter held a monopoly on reality. Then, in the 1830s, Louis Daguerre fixed an image onto a silvered copper plate, and within two generations a camera could render a face more faithfully than any human hand, in a fraction of a second, at a fraction of the cost.

Painting did not die. It asked what it could do that the camera could not, and answered: render not appearance but experience — the simultaneity of perception, the fragmentation of memory, the emotional charge of the unseen. Out of that crisis came Impressionism, Cubism, the entire convulsion of modern art. The lesson runs deep: when a technology destroys an old form of value, the surviving form must move up the stack, into dimensions the machine cannot occupy. Hold that thought; it returns when we speak of AI.

Goya's collapse: the inwardness of horror

The first prophet of this shift painted before photography existed. Francisco de Goya, court painter to four Spanish monarchs, witnessed the Napoleonic invasion of 1808 at first hand. He survived it physically; he did not survive it psychologically. In his final decade, deaf and isolated in a house outside Madrid known as the Quinta del Sordo, Goya painted directly on the plaster walls what we now call the Black Paintings — not to be exhibited, but to be exorcised.

It is not a mythological scene. It is the recognition that Enlightenment Europe, the civilization of Mozart and Voltaire, has been shattered — by the violence of its own people.

Picasso's dimensions: rendering the simultaneous

A century later, Pablo Picasso took up Goya's broken inheritance and did something Goya could not have imagined. He recognized that a face, a city, an event is never seen from one angle alone: we perceive in time, memory layered onto vision, the back of the head as real as the front. Cubism shows all these dimensions at once on a single flat surface — painting's response to a world where photography already does the front view perfectly.

In this legendary canvas, Picasso demonstrates the power of rendering multiple dimensions. The woman sees herself in the mirror not as a simple reflection, but as a composite of youth and shadow, container and contained. Same logic of Cubist superposition: refuse the single point of view, showing the physical form and the internal psychological reality simultaneously.

Guernica, and the two roads out of the ashes

On the afternoon of April 26th, 1937, the German Luftwaffe — at the invitation of Franco's nationalist forces — bombed the Basque market town of Guernica. A Monday. Market day. The town had no military value. The bombing was an experiment: the Condor Legion wanted to study what saturation bombing of civilians would do. The lessons learned at Guernica would be applied two years later to Warsaw, and then to every city in Europe.

Picasso painted his response in a single feverish month. Guernica is the synthesis of everything just described: Goya's black emotional truth, and Cubism's simultaneous dimensions. The screaming horse, the dead child in the mother's arms, the bull, the bare electric bulb hanging like an eye of god — none of it realistic, all of it true.

Out of those ashes, the Basque people had two roads. One led to destruction — the armed struggle that congealed into ETA, four decades of violence whose wreckage is still being cleaned up. The other led to creation — to a young priest who, arriving in 1941 in a town where children were dying of hunger, decided that the only sustainable answer to a civilization that bombs market towns is a community whose bonds are not material but moral. That priest was José María Arizmendiarrieta. His response was Mondragón.

Anchor to Mondragón

When you walk into the cooperative today, remember: you are not entering a business-school case study. You are entering one of the two answers a wounded people gave to Guernica. The other answer was a forty-year armed struggle. That the cooperative road has so dramatically outlasted the violent one is not an accident of history. It is the proposition itself.

Castles and valleys: two anthropologies

To understand why Mondragón flourished in the Basque hills and not on the Castilian plain, hold two landscapes in mind. Castile is open, dry, vast — a land of horizons. Its medieval response to insecurity was the castle: a single armored point of refuge, ruled by a lord, defending a hierarchy. The very name of the region means "land of castles." Castilian culture organized itself around the vertical line of feudal authority.

The Basque valleys offered no such geometry. Narrow, wet, green, turned in on themselves. The architecture of survival was not the castle but the baserri, the farmhouse, and behind it the auzolan — the obligation of mutual labor, the custom that when one house needs a roof rebuilt, every house sends a pair of hands. Auzolan is to the Basque what Pashtunwali is to the Pashtun: an unwritten constitution of solidarity. Mondragón, in 1956, did not invent it. Mondragón industrialized it.

Part IIIEducation as the Engine of Resilience — From the Printing Press to AI

Here is a fact most business books about Mondragón do not emphasize enough: Mondragón did not begin as a factory. It began as a school. In 1943, more than a decade before the first cooperative was founded, Father Arizmendiarrieta opened a small vocational school in a town with no industrial future. He had no capital and no investors — only a conviction, drawn from Catholic social teaching and European cooperative thinkers, that no factory built on uneducated workers can be a factory of free people. Education was the seed; the factory was the fruit. ULGOR, the first cooperative, did not appear until 1956, when five graduates of that school pooled their savings to make paraffin heaters. Today the Mondragón group runs its own university. Education is not an HR function. It is the operating system.

The Gutenberg threshold

Arizmendiarrieta's intuition — that education is the precondition of liberty — is older than the modern world. It is the engine of it. Around 1455, in Mainz, Johannes Gutenberg pressed inked metal type onto paper. Within two generations the cost of a book collapsed by a factor of three hundred. Within four, Europe was a different civilization.

The printing press dissolved the medieval monopoly on interpretation. When a peasant in Saxony could, for the first time, read scripture for himself in his own language, the entire architecture of religious authority cracked. The Reformation followed. The wars of religion. The scientific revolution. The Industrial Revolution. The modern nation-state — needing literate citizens to staff its bureaucracies and armies. Every one of these consequences was downstream of a single cognitive technology that put information into individual hands.

Montaigne, Descartes, and the courage to doubt

Stefan Zweig, in his luminous biography of Michel de Montaigne, captured the human face of this transformation. Withdrawn into his tower at Saint-Michel-de-Montaigne while the wars of religion burned around him, Montaigne dared to do what no medieval scholar would have permitted himself: he wrote down his contradictions, his uncertainties, his changing moods, and called the result Essais — "attempts." He could do this only because Erasmus had taught Europe to read the classics with skeptical eyes; and Erasmus could do that only because Gutenberg had made books cheap.

From Montaigne's I do not know there is a direct line to Descartes's I doubt, therefore I think; I think, therefore I am. Cartesian doubt is not an exotic philosophical exercise. It is the cognitive habit of a literate civilization that has internalized the idea that authority can be wrong. The scientific method is its institutional form. The cooperative, two centuries later, is its industrial form: a factory in which the workers themselves are the court of appeal.

The AI parallel: efficiency without intelligence

We are now living through another Gutenberg moment. Large language models represent a cognitive technology whose social consequences will rival those of movable type. They draft your emails, summarize your readings, argue with you at three in the morning when no human friend is awake. Extraordinary engines.

And yet I want to put to you, gently, a thesis that may sound paradoxical: we are living in the century of efficiency, not the century of intelligence. These systems are unprecedentedly good at producing fluent, plausible output at zero marginal cost. They are not, in any deep sense, thinking. They are pattern-completing at industrial scale. The danger is not that they replace human thought; it is that human thought lowers itself to compete on their terms — that we become as fluent, as plausible, and as shallow as our own machines.

A Note on the Same Stack-Up Problem

Recall what painting did when photography arrived: it did not compete on realism. It moved up the stack, into simultaneity, emotion, multi-dimensional truth — registers the camera could not reach. Human cognition, faced with LLMs, must perform the same migration. The registers the machine cannot reach are humor, provocation, schematization, ethical judgment, the courage to ask the unexpected question and to sit with not-knowing. These are not soft skills. They are the only skills left that are entirely ours.

Mondragón is the institutional rebuttal to algorithmic civilization. It says: the firm exists for the human, not the human for the firm. Capital is a tool, not a master. None of this can be derived from a profit-maximizing algorithm. All of it can be derived from a half-decent vocational school run by a stubborn priest who believed persona is older than capital.

Part IVThe European Competitiveness Trap — Scaling the Mondragón Spirit

Mondragón is not a fairy tale. It is an industrial federation under genuine strain. In 2013 its flagship cooperative Fagor Electrodomésticos — once Spain's largest household appliance maker — went bankrupt. The remaining cooperatives absorbed the displaced workers, retraining and redeploying them rather than letting them fall into the labor market. Costly. Also the system functioning as designed. Mondragón's struggle against low-cost Asian manufacturing, hyperscale American platforms, and vertically-integrated European multinationals is a microcosm of the entire European predicament.

Draghi and Letta: the diagnosis

The Draghi Report of September 2024 delivered a brutal message: Europe's productivity has been falling behind the United States for two decades. The continent has produced almost none of the world's hyperscale technology companies. Its largest firms are in fashion (LVMH), apparel logistics (Inditex), banking, pharmaceuticals — venerable industries, but not the platforms that will dominate the next cycle. Without an investment surge of roughly 800 billion euros a year, Draghi concludes, Europe will become a museum: charming, well-curated, economically irrelevant.

Enrico Letta's parallel report on the Single Market, published April 2024, offers the structural complement. Letta argues that Jacques Delors's Single Market of the 1980s was completed for goods but never finished for capital, energy, telecoms, or services. A European startup cannot raise capital across borders as an American one can. A European energy grid cannot move electricity efficiently from the Friesian coast to a Polish industrial hinterland. A European telecom operator, blocked from continental scale, cannot match the investment power of its rivals.

The language barrier — literal and metaphorical

Beneath both reports lies a problem no policy memo can name. Europe does not speak the same language. I mean this literally — twenty-four official tongues — but I mean it more deeply. A common currency, but no common fiscal authority. A common parliament, but national publics. A common foreign policy on paper, twenty-seven in practice.

"If I want to call Europe, who answers the phone?" — Henry Kissinger (attributed)

The line is half a century old and still painfully accurate. Europe under-performs against its size, education, and accumulated wealth not because Europeans are less talented, but because the institutions that should aggregate that talent are fragmented along lines history has not given Europeans the courage to erase.

Airbus and CERN: what Europe does when it remembers itself

And yet Europe has proof of its own capacity. When it chooses to cooperate, it leads. Airbus, founded in 1970 as a French-German-Spanish-British consortium, today shares the global aviation market in a duopoly with Boeing — and in recent years has out-delivered its American rival. No single European country could have built it. CERN, established near Geneva in 1954 by twelve founding states, is the world's largest particle physics laboratory: where the Higgs boson was confirmed in 2012, and where Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web in 1989 — a side product of European cooperation that Americans commercialized brilliantly, but which Europeans gave to the world for free.

Anchor to Mondragón

Airbus and CERN are Mondragón written at continental scale — proof that the cooperative principle (many actors pooling capital, talent, and risk into a structure no individual could build alone) is not a Basque curiosity. It is one of Europe's most powerful and least imitated forms of competitive advantage. The question Draghi and Letta are really asking is this: can Europe industrialize the Mondragón principle fast enough to remain a serious actor in the twenty-first century?

Part VPhilosophical Epilogue — Your Responsibility in Mondragón Today

In a few hours you will descend from your bus into a town of fewer than twenty-five thousand, in a valley most of your countrymen could not locate on a map. You will be shown a factory floor. A university. A bank — the Caja Laboral Popular — that the cooperatives themselves founded in 1959 so they would never be at the mercy of a banker who did not share their purpose. You will hear an executive in a sober suit explain the assembly governance of a worker-owned multinational. If you are not paying attention, you may find it unremarkable.

Pay attention.

You will be looking at something that, by every reasonable expectation of twentieth-century economic theory, should not exist. A worker-governed firm scaled to seventy thousand employees, surviving the bankruptcy of one of its largest cooperatives without mass layoffs, operating profitably in markets dominated by shareholder capitalism. The textbooks said this was impossible. The textbooks were wrong — because they did not factor in a priest who, in a town starving in 1941, decided that the only durable response to a bombed civilian population was to teach its children how to govern their own work.

This is the deepest lesson I can offer. The future is not a predetermined algorithm. It is not the output of a productivity function. It is not the asymptote of a trend line. The future is a series of decisions taken by named human beings in concrete places at definite times — Arizmendiarrieta in 1941, the founders of Airbus in 1970, the twelve states that signed the CERN convention in 1954 — decisions that, from the dark moment of their inception, looked impossible.

You are arriving at one of those places. You will be tempted, as your fathers and grandfathers were, by two opposite paths: the road of destruction — cynicism, withdrawal, the comfortable nihilism of the over-informed; or the road of creation — the slow, patient, often unglamorous work of building institutions that outlast their builders.

As you walk through Mondragón today — in a world increasingly complex, automated, and hyper-financialized — what will you take from this cooperative miracle to construct the response that your time demands of you?

I do not know what your answer will be. Neither does the priest who started it, dead since 1976. Neither do the seventy thousand cooperators waiting on the other side of the bus ride. The answer is yours to write. The privilege of being twenty-something in 2026 is that you still get to choose. Choose well. The valleys are watching.
